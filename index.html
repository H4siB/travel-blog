My Travel Blog: Bahamas

My experience~
When I think of paradise on Earth, I think of the Bahamas. It’s got everything you could want in a tropical getaway and then some! Beautiful views, crystal clear turquoise waters, white sand as soft as a blanket, beach resorts where are all of your problems will seemingly melt away, and even beaches with pigs.

How can you top that?!

With over 700 islands, there’s an island for every traveler out there. Whether you’re looking to sip Bahama Mamas the entire time or you want to explore the country from the ocean floors, there’s an adventure at every turn in the Bahamas.

Best Time to Visit~
The best time to visit the Bahamas is during the peak season, which runs from December to April. Though the weather is practically perfect every season, the islands do lie within the hurricane belt, so it’s best to avoid them between June and November.

Nothing like a hurricane to ruin your vacation.

Unfortunately, because the weather is perfect in during winter and spring, hordes of tourists make their way to the Bahamas. Prices for hotels and flights are higher, and the beaches are filled with people.

Luckily the waves crashing against the white sand beaches with the sun make it all worthwhile.

What To Expect~

Language: English is the official language of the Bahamas and is spoken by a majority of the population.

Currency: The official currency of the Bahamas is the Bahamian Dollar (BSD). 1 USD is equivalent to 1 BSD. This exchange rate makes everything extremely to understand, and you won’t have to worry about doing too much math.

Credit Cards & ATMs: Credit cards are widely accepted at many restaurants, large shops, and hotels, though it all depends on the location. The same holds true for ATMs. On larger islands such as New Providence Island and Paradise, you will have no trouble finding an ATM. If you’re visiting the Out Islands, you might struggle to find an ATM so you’ll want to make arrangements before you arrive.

Plugs: On the Bahamas, the plugs are type A and B, the standard voltage is 120 V, and the standard frequency is 60 Hz. If visiting from the states, you won’t need an adapter or converter. I recommend buying a universal adapter (make sure it has surge protection) and using a converter for hairdryers and hot tools.

Safety: Because the Bahamas is comprised of over 700 islands, it’s hard to say if the country is safe as a whole. It all varies on the island. For example, Nassau has been known for armed robberies and theft towards tourists. Yet on the Out Islands, this criminal activity is seen far less. To avoid becoming a victim of crime, exercise caution, and watch over any valuables you have with you.

What to Buy?

  1. Rum: As the birthplace of rum, Barbados boasts some of the finest bottles in the world. Brands like Mount Gay, Foursquare and Cockspur offer tours and tastings, where you can learn about the distillation process and pick up a bottle or two of their premium blends.
  2. Bajan Hot Sauce: Spice up your meals with a bottle of fiery Bajan hot sauce. Made from Scotch bonnet peppers, mustard and vinegar, this sauce is a staple in Barbadian cuisine and a must-have souvenir for spice lovers.
  3. Local Pottery: Barbados has a thriving pottery industry, with local artisans creating beautiful, handcrafted pieces that reflect the island's culture and natural beauty. Look for items like clay pots, decorative plates and garden ornaments.
  4. Mahogany Wood Crafts: The rich, dark mahogany wood is a hallmark of Barbadian craftsmanship. Skilled artisans carve this durable wood into elegant furniture, sculptures and trinkets, each piece showcasing the exquisite workmanship of local craftsmen.
  5. Barbadian Music: Bring home the rhythmic sounds of calypso, soca or reggae with CDs and vinyl records from local music shops. Barbadian music captures the spirit of the island and keeps the festive vibes going long after your vacation ends.
  6. Flying Fish Art: Flying fish, a national symbol of Barbados, inspire various forms of artwork and souvenirs, from paintings and sculptures to printed fabrics and t-shirts. These make for unique gifts that embody a quintessential aspect of Barbadian culture.
  7. Jewelry Made From Local Stones: Local artisans often use pink coral, amber and other indigenous materials to create stunning pieces of jewelry. These unique accessories are a wonderful way to carry a piece of the island with you wherever you go.
  8. Sugar & Molasses: Barbados was historically known as "the sugar island." Local sugars and molasses can be found in specialty food shops and are fantastic for those who love to bake or enjoy a rich, sweet flavor in their cooking.
  9. Cane Sugar Rum Cake: Indulge in this deliciously moist rum cake made with local cane sugar and rum. It’s a perfect Barbadian souvenir ideal for sharing with friends and family — or savoring a slice with your afternoon tea.
  10. Beachwear: With its endless summer climate, Barbados is the perfect place to buy stylish beachwear. From hand-printed sarongs to designer swimwear, you’ll find everything you need to hit the beaches in style.

What I Ate?

  • Flying Fish & Cou-Cou: This national dish of Barbados is a must-try for any visitor. Flying fish, a local delicacy, is lightly seasoned, steamed or fried, then served alongside cou-cou, a side dish made from cornmeal and okra. This dish is often paired with a flavorful tomato-based sauce for a true taste of Bajan cuisine.
  • Pudding & Souse: One of the best foods in Barbados, pudding and souse is a traditional Saturday meal made with pickled pork and a spiced sweet potato mash. The pork is marinated with lime juice, cucumbers and onions, giving it a tangy and refreshing taste. This dish is a staple at local markets and is best enjoyed with a cold drink.
  • Bajan Macaroni Pie: If you love mac and cheese, you’ll love Bajan macaroni pie. This Caribbean version of baked mac and cheese is made with elbow macaroni, eggs, evaporated milk, mustard and cheddar cheese, then baked to perfection. It’s often served as a side with fried fish or jerk chicken.
  • Fish Cakes: Fish cakes are one of the most popular foods in Barbados. These golden-fried delights are made from salted cod, flour and herbs, then deep-fried until crispy. They’re often served with a spicy Bajan pepper sauce and make for a perfect snack or appetizer.
  • Breadfruit: A versatile Caribbean staple, breadfruit is a starchy fruit that can be roasted, boiled or fried. In Barbados, you’ll often find it served as roasted breadfruit chips or mashed and served alongside stews. It’s a delicious and filling dish with a mild, nutty flavor.
  • Cutters: A cutter is a Bajan sandwich made with salt bread, a slightly dense and chewy roll, filled with meats such as flying fish, ham or cheese. The most famous version is the flying fish cutter, served with lettuce, tomato and pepper sauce for an extra kick.
  • Conkies: This traditional dish is made from grated pumpkin, cornmeal, coconut, sweet potatoes and spices, all wrapped in banana leaves and steamed. Conkies are especially popular during Barbados’ Independence celebrations in November and are a sweet, comforting treat.
  • Pepperpot: This traditional dish is made from grated pumpkin, cornmeal, coconut, sweet potatoes and spices, all wrapped in banana leaves and steamed. Conkies are especially popular during Barbados’ Independence celebrations in November and are a sweet, comforting treat.
  • Rum Cake: This traditional dish is made from grated pumpkin, cornmeal, coconut, sweet potatoes and spices, all wrapped in banana leaves and steamed. Conkies are especially popular during Barbados’ Independence celebrations in November and are a sweet, comforting treat.
  • Tamarind Balls: For a sweet and tangy treat, try tamarind balls — a simple, yet addictive snack made from tamarind pulp, mixed with sugar and rolled into bite-sized balls. These chewy, slightly sour candies are a favorite among locals and visitors alike.
